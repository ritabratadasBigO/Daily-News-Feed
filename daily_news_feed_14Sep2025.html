Published Date: Sat, 13 Sep 2025

Japan Post Bank, the country's largest state-owned financial institution, will launch "tokenized deposits" in fiscal year 2026, marking a significant step for blockchain adoption in Japanese finance. This service will allow customers to convert their existing savings into digital tokens linked to the "digital yen (DCJPY)," a platform developed by DeCurret DCP. Tokenized deposits will be held in a separate account and can be used for payments or trading financial products like real estate investment securities and digital art. While interest will not be paid on these deposits, they will be covered by deposit insurance, ensuring customer safety. This move follows similar initiatives by global financial institutions such as JPMorgan Chase, Citigroup, and DBS Bank, indicating a growing trend in tokenized assets. The Boston Consulting Group predicts the tokenized asset market will experience substantial growth, reaching 26.292 trillion by 2033.

Image not found.Hospitals, insurers face off: GI Council cautions against cashless care disruption - The Indian Express

Published Date: Sat, 13 Sep 2025

Tensions are escalating between hospitals and insurance companies in India, with the Association of Healthcare Providers (India) (AHPI) threatening to suspend cashless services for Star Health Insurance policyholders. AHPI, representing over 15,000 hospitals, cites unresolved grievances with Star Health, including claim rejections, payment disputes, and alleged unfair tariff negotiations. The General Insurance Council (GI Council), representing non-life insurers, strongly opposes AHPI's stance, arguing that it jeopardizes patient care and trust in the health insurance ecosystem. The GI Council emphasizes that Star Health policyholders will continue to have full coverage and access to treatment, despite the threat. This dispute highlights the broader challenge of rising medical costs and insurance payouts, with hospitals demanding higher rates and insurers seeking to control costs. The situation underscores the need for constructive dialogue and compromise between hospitals and insurers to ensure patient well-being and a stable health insurance system.
